Dual Theory of LP#

Origin of dual problem#

Consider a LP with standard form:

$$ \begin{align*} \min\ & c^\top x\\ \text{s.t. } & Ax=b\\ & x\geq 0 \end{align*} $$

We can surrogate the objective function by introducing constraint penalty:

$$ \begin{align*} \min_x\ & c^\top x+\lambda^\top(b-Ax)\\ \text{s.t. } & x\geq 0 \end{align*} $$

Denote $g(\lambda):=c^\top x+\lambda^\top(b-Ax)$, then $g(\lambda)\leq f(x)$ for all $\lambda$. Therefore, we need to maxmize $g(\lambda)$ to obtain the tightest lower bound. Notice that

$$ g(\lambda)=(c^\top-\lambda^\top A)x+\lambda^\top b= \begin{cases} -\infty, & \text{if }c^\top-\lambda^\top A<0;\\ \lambda^\top b, & \text{if }c^\top-\lambda^\top A\geq 0. \end{cases} $$

then the dual problem is

$$ \begin{align*} \max\ & \lambda^\top b\\ \text{s.t. } & \lambda^\top A\leq c^\top \end{align*} $$

Properties of dual#

Suppose $x^*$ and $p^*$ are the optimal solution of (P) and (D) respectively.

  • Weak duality: $c^\top x^*\geq p^{*\top}b$.

  • Strong duality: If $x^*$ is finite, then $p^*$ is finite, and $c^\top x^*= p^{*\top}b$.

    • Corollary: (P) unbounded $\implies$ (D) infeasible, (P) infeasible $\implies$ (D) unbounded or infeasible.

  • Complementary slackness: $(x^*,p^*)$ is optimal $\iff p_i^*(a_i^\top x^*-b)=0$ and $(c_j-p_j^{*\top}A_j)x^*=0$.

Lagrangian duality#

Consider the following problem:

$$ \begin{align*} p^*:=\min\ & f(x)\\ \text{s.t. } & g_i(x)\leq 0,\ i\in[m];\\ & h_j(x)=0,\ j\in[n]. \end{align*} $$

Its Lagrangian function is defined as

$$ L(\lambda,\mu)=\inf_x\left\lbrace f(x)+\sum_{i\in[m]}\lambda_i g_i(x)+\sum_{j\in[n]}\mu_j h_j(x)\right\rbrace $$

Denote $d^*:=\sup\limits_{\lambda,\mu}L(\lambda,\mu)$, then $p^*\geq d^*$ by weak duality.

If $f(x)$ is convex and local minimum satisfies some constraint qualifications (abbreviated as CQs), then strong duality will hold.

e.g. Slater condition, LICQ, MFCQ…

Note: The Lagrangian dual is always convex, and sometimes it is easier to be tackled.


Def. If $(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)$ satisfies

$$ \sup_{\lambda,\mu\geq 0}L(x^*,\lambda,\mu)\leq L(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*) \leq \inf_{x}L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*) $$

,then it is called a saddle point.

Thm. $(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)$ is optimal $\iff (x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)$ is a saddle point.

Content of KKT condition#

  • $g_i(x^*)\leq 0,\ i\in[m]$ (primal feasibility)

  • $h_j(x^*)=0,\ j\in[n]$ (primal feasibility)

  • $\lambda_i^*\geq 0,\ i\in[m]$ (dual feasibility)

  • $\mu_j^*\in\mathbb{R},\ j\in[n]$ (dual feasibility)

  • $\nabla f(x^*)+\sum_{i\in[m]}\lambda^*_i \nabla g_i(x^*)+\sum_{j\in[n]}\mu^*_j \nabla h_j(x^*)=\mathbf{0}$ (FOC of Lagrangian function)

  • $\lambda^{*\top}g(x^*)=0$ (complementary slackness)

optimal solution $\implies$ KKT point is obvious, but the opposite direction is not easy to prove. But fortunately, if $f(x)$ is convex, then opposite direction holds.

Conic dual#

The rule of constructing LP’s dual problem can be easily applied on conic programming:

$$ \begin{align*} \text{(P) } \min\ & c^\top x\\ \text{s.t. } & Ax\succcurlyeq_{\mathcal{K}}b \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \text{(D) } \min\ & p^\top b\\ \text{s.t. } & p^\top A=c^\top\\ & p\succcurlyeq_{\mathcal{K^*}}0 \end{align*} $$

where $Ax\succcurlyeq_{\mathcal{K}}b$ means $Ax-b\in\mathcal{K}$ ($\mathcal{K}$ is a cone), and $\mathcal{K}^*:=\lbrace y\ |\ y^\top x\geq 0,\ x\in\mathcal{K}\rbrace$ denotes the dual cone.
